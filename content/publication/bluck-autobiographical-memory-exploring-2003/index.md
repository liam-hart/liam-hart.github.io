abstract: 'This special issue of Memory spotlights research that uses a functional
  approach to investigate autobiographical memory (AM) in everyday life. This approach
  relies on studying cognition, in this case AM, taking into account the psychological,
  social, or cultural-historic context in which it occurs. Areas of interest include
  understanding to what ends AM is used by individuals and in social relationships,
  how it is related to other cognitive abilities and emotional states, and how memory
  represents our inner and outer world. One insight gained by taking this approach
  is that levels and types of accuracy need not always be regarded as memory \"failures\"
  but are sometimes integral to a self-memory system that serves a variety of meaningful
  ends of human activity. Previously hypothesised functions of AM fall into three
  broad domains: self, social, and directive. Each of the contributions addresses
  how AM serves one or more of these functions and thereby examines the usefulness
  and adequacy of this trio.'

abstract: New discoveries challenge the long-held view that aging is characterized
  by progressive loss and decline. Evidence for functional reorganization, compensation
  and effective interventions holds promise for a more optimistic view of neurocognitive
  status in later life. Complexities associated with assigning function to age-specific
  activation patterns must be considered relative to performance and in light of pathological
  aging. New biological and genetic markers, coupled with advances in imaging technologies,
  are enabling more precise characterization of healthy aging. This interdisciplinary,
  cognitive neuroscience approach reveals dynamic and optimizing processes in aging
  that might be harnessed to foster the successful aging of the mind.

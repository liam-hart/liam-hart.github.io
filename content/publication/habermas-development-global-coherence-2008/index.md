title: 'The Development of Global Coherence in Life Narratives across Adolescence:
  Temporal, Causal, and Thematic Aspects'
authors:
- Tilmann Habermas
- Cybèle De Silveira
date: '2008-05-01'
publishDate: '2025-05-28T02:32:39.644744Z'
publication_types:
- article-journal
publication: '*Developmental Psychology*'
doi: 10.1037/0012-1649.44.3.707
abstract: Extending the study of autobiographical narratives to entire life narratives,
  we tested the emergence of globally coherent life narratives in adolescence, as
  hypothesized by McAdams (1985). Participants were 102 children and young adults
  (ages 8, 12, 16, and 20 years) who narrated their lives twice. Between narrations,
  half of each age group participated in tasks designed to train autobiographical
  reasoning; the other half participated in control tasks. Coherence was measured
  by the relative frequency of local temporal, causal, and thematic linguistic indicators
  identified qualitatively at the level of propositions, as well as by quantitative
  global rating scales measuring the impressions of the listeners. Coherence increased
  across the age span. Overall, repeated narrating and training did not increase coherence.
  Crystallized and fluid intelligence, number of negative life events, and frequency
  of biographical practices and confiding in others did not contribute substantially
  to the prediction of coherence beyond age. Results are interpreted in the context
  of adolescent identity development.

title: Meaning Making in Mothers' and Children's Narratives of Emotional Events
authors:
- Robyn Fivush
- Jessica McDermott Sales
- Jennifer G. Bohanek
date: '2008-08-01'
publishDate: '2025-05-28T02:32:39.188220Z'
publication_types:
- article-journal
publication: '*Memory*'
doi: 10.1080/09658210802150681
abstract: Narrative coherence and the inclusion of mental state language are critical
  aspects of meaning making, especially about stressful events. Mothers and their
  8- to 12-year-old children with asthma independently narrated a time they were scared,
  frustrated, and happy. Although mothers' narratives were generally more coherent
  and more saturated with mental state language than children's narratives, for both
  mothers and children narratives of negative events were more coherent and contained
  more mental state language than narratives of positive events overall, and narratives
  of scary events contained more mental state language than narratives of frustrating
  events. Coherence appears to be multifaceted, in that the three dimensions of coherence
  coded, context, chronology, and theme were not strongly interrelated within narratives
  of the same event, but use of mental state language, including cognitive-processing
  and emotion words, appears to be more integrated. Moreover, while thematic coherence
  seems to be a consistent individual narrative style across valence of event being
  narrated, mental state language appears to be a consistent style only across the
  two stressful event narratives. Finally, and quite surprisingly, there were virtually
  no relations between mothers' and children's narrative meaning making.

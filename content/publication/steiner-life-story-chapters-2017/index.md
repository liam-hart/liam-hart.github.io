abstract: Summary             Two studies investigated the effects of recalling either
  life story chapters or specific memories on measures of self-continuity and self-esteem.
  Participants were assigned to recall important chapters, important specific memories,
  or impersonal facts, and they provided ratings of emotional tone. Participants also
  completed trait and state measures of self-continuity, self-esteem, and mood. Although
  effects of recall condition on state and trait measures were not statistically significant,
  within-group analyses identified strong and consistent relationships between the
  positivity of life story chapters and both trait and state self-continuity and self-esteem.
  In contrast, the positivity of specific memories was related only to state self-esteem.
  Qualities of life story chapters appear to be more central to enduring conceptions
  of the self than do qualities of specific life story memories.Copyright o̧pyright

abstract: Though emotion conveys memory benefits, it does not enhance memory equally
  for all aspects of an experience, nor for all types of emotional events. In this
  review, I outline the behavioral evidence for arousal's focal enhancements of memory
  and describe the neural processes that may support those focal enhancements. I also
  present behavioral evidence to suggest that these focal enhancements occur more
  often for negative experiences than for positive ones. This result appears to arise
  because of valence-dependent effects on the neural processes recruited during episodic
  encoding and retrieval, with negative affect associated with increased engagement
  of sensory processes, and positive affect leading to enhanced recruitment of conceptual
  processes.

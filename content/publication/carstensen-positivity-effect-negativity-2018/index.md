publication: '*Current Opinion in Behavioral Sciences*'
doi: 10.1016/j.cobeha.2017.07.009
abstract: Relative to younger adults, older adults attend to and remember positive
  information more than negative information. This shift from a negativity bias in
  younger age to a preference for positive information in later life is termed the
  'positivity effect.' Based on nearly two decades of research and recent evidence
  from neuroscience, we argue that the effect reflects age-related changes in motivation
  that direct behavior and cognitive processing rather than neural or cognitive decline.
  Understanding the positivity effect, including conditions that reduce and enhance
  it, can inform effective public health and educational messages directed at older
  people.

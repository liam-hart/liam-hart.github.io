publication: '*Psychology and Aging*'
doi: 10.1037/0882-7974.8.2.165
abstract: The self-reports of 207 young-adult (ages 18-30), 231 middle-aged (ages
  31-59), and 828 older-adult (age 60 and over) Ss were used to study the structure
  of affect. Affects were represented by terms included in various circumplex arrays
  of emotions as presented by previous investigators. A set of 46 affects was subjected
  to exploratory analysis, and a final set of 38 affects was subjected to confirmatory
  factor analysis. The goodness of fit of each group's factor loadings to the hypothesized
  factors of positive affect, depression, anxiety-guilt, contentment, hostility, and
  shyness was not up to the desired .90 level, and some significant differences in
  factor structure were observed for each age-group comparison. There were few age
  differences in levels of positive affect. Depression was most frequent among younger
  Ss and least frequent among older Ss. Younger Ss were most often anxious and shy.
  Older Ss were most often content and least often hostile.
